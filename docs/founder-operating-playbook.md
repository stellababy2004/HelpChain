# HelpChain Founder Operating Playbook

Internal founder/operator manual for running HelpChain as an early-stage, France-first B2B SaaS product.

This document is not public positioning. It is the operating system for weekly execution: find qualified institutions, run disciplined demos, convert pilots, and tie product work to revenue.

## 1. Founder Mission Control

The founder manages four engines every week.

### 1. Product Engine

- Keep HelpChain stable, credible, and demo-ready.
- Prioritize product work that removes a buyer objection or improves pilot activation.
- Protect the Request workflow, admin visibility, onboarding, and telemetry surfaces.
- Avoid building features without a named prospect, pilot, or revenue reason.

### 2. Pipeline Engine

- Create new qualified conversations every week.
- Track every target, contact, reply, demo, pilot, and next action.
- Treat silence as a follow-up problem, not as a decision.
- Use audience intelligence to detect where market interest is already appearing.

### 3. Pilot Engine

- Convert demos into short, concrete pilots.
- Define pilot scope, users, success criteria, and review date.
- Keep pilots small enough to launch quickly.
- Move pilots toward paid conversion, not endless discovery.

### 4. Trust Engine

- Sell credibility: operational clarity, traceability, tenant scoping, admin control, and institutional seriousness.
- Make security, access control, and data boundaries easy to explain.
- Document objections and turn repeated objections into either product fixes or founder talk tracks.
- Keep demos calm, precise, and grounded in real public-interest workflows.

## 2. Daily Founder Dashboard

Review this every morning before product work.

- New organization access requests: `/admin/organizations/requests`
- New professional leads: `/admin/professional-leads`
- Audience Radar hot sessions: `/admin/audience-map`
- Top viewed pages: especially `/offre`, `/deploiement`, `/professionnels`, `/demander-acces`, `/contact`
- Repeat visitors: sessions returning to high-intent pages
- Pending follow-ups: anyone with no next action or stale last contact
- Pilot opportunities: demos completed, pilots proposed, warm institutional conversations
- Blocked product issues: anything that would break a demo, pilot, onboarding, or trust conversation

Morning rule: do not start feature work until revenue signals and follow-ups have been reviewed.

## 3. Sales OS: Founder Pipeline Discipline

Use a simple pipeline. Keep it in the product if available, otherwise in a spreadsheet until the workflow deserves deeper productization.

### Pipeline Stages

- Target identified
- Contacted
- Conversation started
- Demo booked
- Demo completed
- Pilot proposed
- Pilot active
- Paying client
- Lost / paused

### Recommended Columns

| Org | Ville | Type | Stage | Last Contact | Next Action | Score | Value |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| CCAS example | Boulogne-Billancourt | CCAS | Demo booked | 2026-04-23 | Send agenda | Hot | Pilot |

### Operating Rules

- Every active prospect must have a next action.
- Every next action must have a date.
- Every demo must end with a booked follow-up or a clear close/lost reason.
- Every lost/paused prospect must have one reason recorded.
- Any prospect with high audience intent and no contact attempt becomes a same-day action.

## 4. Prospecting Rhythm: France-First

Weekly minimum targets:

- 20 new targets identified per week
- 10 follow-ups per week
- 3 calls booked per week target
- 1 demo per week minimum

### Priority Territories

- Hauts-de-Seine
- Paris
- Ile-de-France

### Priority Buyer / Partner Types

- CCAS
- Centres sociaux
- Associations structurees
- Collectivites
- Local institutional networks
- Professionals who influence social, care, or coordination workflows

### Weekly Outreach Blocks

- Monday: build target list and send first wave.
- Tuesday: follow up on previous week and respond to warm signals.
- Wednesday: book calls and run demos.
- Thursday: pilot proposals and stakeholder follow-ups.
- Friday: CEO review, pipeline cleanup, next-week actions.

### Targeting Notes

- Start local and specific before going broad.
- Prefer named operational pain over generic transformation language.
- Look for signs of coordination load: email chains, Excel tracking, phone routing, manual assignment, unclear follow-up.
- Prioritize organizations where a small pilot can be launched without a long procurement cycle.

## 5. Demo Playbook

Run a 20-minute demo. Keep control of time.

### Demo Structure

1. Pain today: ask how requests, assignments, follow-ups, and visibility work now.
2. Current chaos: name the likely tools without judgment: mail, Excel, phone, shared folders, manual relance.
3. HelpChain flow: show request intake, admin triage, assignment, status tracking, and operational follow-up.
4. Admin visibility: show how the admin can see what is pending, assigned, blocked, and completed.
5. Onboarding speed: explain how a structure and first admin can be created from an approved access request.
6. Pilot proposal: suggest the smallest real workflow to test.
7. Next step booked live: schedule the follow-up before the call ends.

### Demo Rules

- Do not over-demo features.
- Do not apologize for early-stage status; frame it as a focused pilot.
- Do not end with "I will send information" unless the next meeting is already booked.
- Ask for the real workflow they would test first.
- Capture objections immediately after the call.

## 6. Pilot Conversion Model

Use simple pilot offers. Pricing can be customized later; the immediate goal is to create a paid or conversion-ready learning loop.

### Pilot Options

- 30-day pilot: fastest test for one team, one workflow, one admin owner.
- 60-day pilot: better for institutional review cycles or multi-person coordination.
- Territory pilot: focused on one city, district, or local service area.
- Association network pilot: one lead association plus a small network of partner users.

### Pilot Definition Checklist

- Named institution or network
- Named operational owner
- Scope of requests/workflows included
- Users involved
- Start date
- Review date
- Success criteria
- Conversion decision date

### Pilot Success Criteria Examples

- Fewer requests tracked by email or spreadsheet.
- Faster assignment visibility.
- Clearer admin follow-up.
- Better traceability of open requests.
- Operators can explain who owns what without manual reconstruction.

## 7. Founder KPIs

Track weekly. Review every Friday.

| KPI | Target / Notes |
| --- | --- |
| New leads | Count all new organization requests, professional leads, and qualified manual targets |
| Replies | Track positive, neutral, and objection replies |
| Demos booked | Meetings scheduled with a real stakeholder |
| Demos held | Completed demos, not just booked calls |
| Pilots launched | Pilots with a named owner and start date |
| Active pilots | Pilots currently running |
| Paid conversions | Pilots or prospects converted to paying clients |
| MRR | Current monthly recurring revenue |
| Time-to-close | Days from first contact to paid/pilot decision |

### KPI Discipline

- If outreach is low, pipeline problems are founder execution problems.
- If demos are low but audience signals are high, follow-up is too weak.
- If demos do not convert to pilots, the offer or buyer pain is unclear.
- If pilots do not convert, success criteria were probably vague or ownership was weak.

## 8. How to Use Existing HelpChain Internal Tools

### `/admin/organizations/requests`

Use this as the institutional intake inbox.

- Review every new access request same day.
- Qualify organization type, city, estimated users, and message.
- Use approve/reject/need-info to keep the queue clean.
- Treat approved requests as onboarding opportunities, not just admin records.

### `/admin/professional-leads`

Use this as the professional and partner lead pipeline.

- Review new leads daily.
- Filter by city, profession, status, and urgency.
- Mark follow-up actions clearly.
- Move qualified professionals toward conversation, referral, or pilot support.

### `/admin/audience-map`

Use this as founder market intelligence.

- Check which public pages are getting attention.
- Watch cities and regions when location data exists.
- Read top pages to understand what buyers are investigating.
- Use founder insights to decide which message or territory to push next.

### Revenue Radar

Use this as the daily hot-session view.

- Prioritize sessions with high scores and hot temperatures.
- Look for repeated visits to `/offre`, `/deploiement`, `/professionnels`, `/demander-acces`, and `/contact`.
- Treat hot anonymous intent as a signal to increase outreach in the relevant segment or territory.
- Use linkage badges to see when a session has already converted into a request or lead.

### Prospect Auto Capture

Use this as conversion context.

- On an access request or lead detail page, read "Audience avant conversion".
- Check pages viewed before conversion.
- Note source, score, temperature, and repeated visit behavior.
- Use this context to tailor the first reply or qualification call.

## 9. Weekly CEO Review: Friday

Answer these questions every Friday.

- What moved revenue this week?
- What blocked growth?
- Which prospects are hottest?
- Which demos need follow-up?
- Which pilots need a conversion action?
- What objections repeated?
- What feature would close a real deal?
- What product work did not connect to revenue?
- What should the founder stop doing next week?

### Friday Outputs

- Updated pipeline stages.
- Top 10 prospects for next week.
- Follow-up list with dates.
- Product priority list tied to buyer evidence.
- One decision to remove low-value work.

## 10. Rules of Founder Discipline

- No week without outreach.
- No demo without a next step.
- No lead without follow-up.
- No pilot without owner, scope, and review date.
- No feature without business reason.
- No product work before checking revenue signals.
- No vanity busywork.
- No vague "interesting conversation" in the pipeline; record stage and next action.
- No custom promise unless it can support repeatable sales.
- No hiding behind product polish when the problem is prospecting.

## Operating Cadence Summary

### Daily

- Check access requests, professional leads, Revenue Radar, top pages, repeat visitors, and follow-ups.
- Send or schedule the next revenue action before deep product work.

### Weekly

- Add 20 new targets.
- Send 10 follow-ups.
- Book 3 calls if possible.
- Run at least 1 demo.
- Propose pilots from qualified demos.
- Run Friday CEO review.

### Monthly

- Review lead sources and conversion quality.
- Decide which segment deserves the next focused push.
- Clean lost/paused prospects.
- Review product priorities against actual revenue movement.

## One-Line Operating Principle

Every week must create evidence: new conversations, clearer buyer pain, stronger pilots, or revenue.
