# First 10 Paying Clients Plan

Internal execution plan for winning the first 10 paying HelpChain clients in the next 90 days.

This plan assumes a solo founder / lean operator motion. The path is direct founder-led sales, small pilots, fast proof, and disciplined follow-up.

## 1. Revenue Objective

The first milestone is not maximum contract value. It is recurring revenue, references, and proof that HelpChain solves a real coordination problem.

### Practical First Revenue Targets

- Option A: 10 clients x EUR300/month = EUR3,000 MRR
- Option B: 5 clients x EUR600/month = EUR3,000 MRR
- Option C: 2 collectivites + 3 associations at mixed pilot pricing

### Founder Objective

Optimize for:

- Speed to first paid usage.
- Local referenceability.
- Clear before/after proof.
- Repeatable sales language.
- Low-friction pilots that can convert.

Do not optimize yet for perfect enterprise packaging, complex procurement, or feature breadth.

## 2. Ideal First Clients

Rank targets by ease, value, urgency, and likelihood of moving within 90 days.

### Tier A: Start Here

- CCAS in Hauts-de-Seine.
- Medium associations with visible coordination pain.
- Local centres sociaux.
- Structures already using email, Excel, phone, and manual relance to manage requests.
- Organizations where one operational owner can sponsor a pilot.

Why Tier A works:

- Pain is concrete.
- Workflows are local and operational.
- Pilots can be scoped narrowly.
- Founder can build geographic credibility quickly.

### Tier B: Pursue Selectively

- Mairie services with a clear social, solidarity, or citizen-support workflow.
- Federations with member organizations that need coordination.
- NGO or association networks that can open several downstream opportunities.

Why Tier B matters:

- Higher network value.
- Better referral potential.
- More stakeholders, so slower than Tier A.

### Tier C: Defer Unless Warm

- Large public bodies with slow procurement.
- Regional or national institutions requiring long validation cycles.
- Buyers demanding heavy customization before a pilot.

Why Tier C is risky now:

- Long sales cycles.
- Procurement drag.
- High trust burden before HelpChain has references.
- Easy to confuse activity with progress.

## 3. Geographic Launch Sequence

### Phase 1: 92 Hauts-de-Seine

Focus first on Hauts-de-Seine.

- Easier local credibility.
- Higher meeting density.
- Better chance of warm referrals.
- Useful first references near the founder's initial launch zone.

### Phase 2: Paris 75

Expand once there are live conversations or early proof in 92.

- Larger buyer pool.
- More associations and social support organizations.
- More competition for attention, so use proof from Phase 1.

### Phase 3: Wider Ile-de-France

Expand to the wider region after pilots or references exist.

- Use early case proof to approach neighboring territories.
- Prioritize organizations with similar workflows to the first successful pilots.

### Why Geographic Density Matters

- References travel faster locally.
- Founder can understand buyer language in one territory.
- Outreach feels less generic.
- Local wins make future demos more credible.
- Pilots can be compared across similar operating contexts.

## 4. Founder Weekly Sales Targets

Weekly minimum:

- 25 new prospects researched.
- 15 outreach contacts sent.
- 8 follow-ups sent.
- 3 calls booked.
- 1 live demo minimum.
- 1 pilot proposal every 2 weeks.

### Weekly Sales Block

- Monday: research 25 prospects and send 8 to 10 first-contact emails.
- Tuesday: send follow-ups and LinkedIn touches.
- Wednesday: call after email for high-fit targets.
- Thursday: run demos, send pilot proposals, book next steps.
- Friday: review pipeline, update stages, plan next week's top targets.

### Pipeline Math

To win 10 paying clients, the founder likely needs:

- 250 to 400 researched prospects.
- 120 to 180 direct outreach attempts.
- 40 to 60 real conversations.
- 15 to 25 demos.
- 8 to 15 pilots.
- 10 paid conversions or paid pilots.

The exact ratios will change. The founder's job is to create enough qualified volume to learn quickly.

## 5. Acquisition Channels: Ranked by ROI

### 1. Founder Direct Email

Best first channel.

- Cheap.
- Fast.
- Controlled message.
- Easy to measure.
- Works well for local, specific institutional outreach.

### 2. Warm Intros

Highest trust channel.

- Ask every relevant contact for one intro.
- Make the ask specific: CCAS director, centre social coordinator, association director, mairie social action lead.
- Provide a short forwardable note.

### 3. LinkedIn Direct Outreach

Useful for identifying and reaching decision-makers.

- Keep messages short.
- Do not pitch the whole product.
- Ask for a quick exchange around coordination pain.

### 4. Phone Call After Email

Use for high-fit targets.

- Call 24 to 72 hours after sending email.
- Reference the email.
- Ask who owns coordination or social support operations.
- Goal is not to sell on the phone; goal is to book a short call.

### 5. Local Events / Association Ecosystem

Use selectively.

- Attend only if the room contains target buyers or referrers.
- Follow up the same day.
- Do not let events replace direct outreach.

### 6. Partnerships / Referrals

Useful after proof.

- Professional networks.
- Local association networks.
- Federations.
- Consultants or operators already trusted by collectivités and associations.

## 7. Demo-to-Close Flow

Use the same structure every time.

1. Understand current coordination pain.
2. Show where fragmented workflows create risk: mail, Excel, phone, unclear ownership, manual relance.
3. Show HelpChain replacing the fragmented workflow with request tracking, assignment, status, and admin visibility.
4. Show pilotage: what is pending, assigned, blocked, completed, and visible to admins.
5. Show onboarding simplicity: access request, structure, first admin, scoped workspace.
6. Offer a narrow pilot.
7. Book the next decision date before ending the meeting.

### Close the Demo With One Question

"Would it make sense to test this on one real workflow for 30 days?"

If yes: define scope and book pilot setup.

If maybe: ask what is missing for a decision.

If no: record the reason and ask for a referral if appropriate.

## 8. Pricing Strategy for First Clients

Pricing should validate seriousness without creating procurement friction.

### Recommended First Pricing

- Discounted early pilot: EUR150 to EUR300/month for small associations or very narrow pilots.
- Standard early pilot: EUR300 to EUR600/month for CCAS, centres sociaux, or structured teams.
- Network / multi-structure pilot: custom monthly price based on scope.

### Pricing Rules

- Charge monthly at first.
- Avoid complex annual contracts initially.
- Keep pilot terms simple.
- Charge enough that the buyer treats the pilot seriously.
- Do not make free pilots the default.
- Discount for speed, referenceability, and clear feedback.

### What to Avoid

- Long procurement before proof.
- Custom pricing spreadsheets.
- Enterprise packaging before 10 clients.
- Promising unlimited customization.
- Confusing pilot price with long-term price.

## 9. Objection Handling

### "We Have No Budget"

Response:

"I understand. That is why I would not propose a large project. The question is whether a small paid pilot can prove enough value on one workflow to justify continuing. If even a small pilot is impossible, it is probably better to reconnect when budget timing is clearer."

### "We Already Use Excel"

Response:

"That makes sense. HelpChain is not replacing Excel because Excel is bad. It is for the point where Excel no longer shows ownership, status, relances, and operational visibility clearly enough. The pilot would test whether that point exists for one workflow."

### "We Are Too Small for Software"

Response:

"Possibly. The question is not size; it is coordination load. If requests are easy to track manually, you may not need HelpChain yet. If follow-up depends on memory, email search, or one person holding the system together, a small pilot may be useful."

### "We Need Internal Approval"

Response:

"Of course. Let us make the approval easy: one workflow, limited duration, clear users, clear review date, and no heavy commitment. Who needs to be involved in that decision?"

### "What About Data and Security?"

Response:

"That is a serious question. HelpChain is designed around controlled access, admin roles, tenant scoping by structure, and traceability. For a pilot, we can define exactly what data enters the system and keep the scope limited."

### "No Time Now"

Response:

"Understood. Should I reconnect in [two weeks / next month], or is there another person closer to the operational follow-up who should see it first?"

## 11. 90-Day Execution Plan

### Month 1: Pipeline and Demos

Goal: create enough qualified conversations to learn.

Actions:

- Build a list of 100 to 150 Tier A prospects in Hauts-de-Seine.
- Send 60 to 80 founder-led outreach messages.
- Follow up at least twice with high-fit targets.
- Book 8 to 12 calls.
- Run 4 to 6 demos.
- Document objections after every call.
- Keep product work limited to demo blockers and buyer objections.

Success by end of Month 1:

- Clear segment response data.
- At least 4 demos completed.
- At least 2 serious pilot opportunities.
- One sharpened demo narrative.

### Month 2: Pilots Launched

Goal: turn demos into real usage.

Actions:

- Launch 2 to 4 paid or conversion-ready pilots.
- Continue weekly outreach; do not pause prospecting.
- Add Paris targets once 92 outreach is active.
- Collect before/after proof from pilots.
- Tighten onboarding steps for each pilot.
- Ask every pilot sponsor for one referral.

Success by end of Month 2:

- 2 or more active pilots.
- At least one paid pilot.
- Repeatable pilot proposal.
- Better objection handling.

### Month 3: Convert Pilots and Referrals

Goal: convert active pilots and expand through proof.

Actions:

- Hold pilot review meetings.
- Convert successful pilots to monthly paid clients.
- Ask for references, introductions, or permission to describe the result privately.
- Use first proof to approach similar organizations.
- Push wider Ile-de-France only where the message is now validated.
- Keep closing discipline: every warm account needs a next decision date.

Success by end of Month 3:

- 3 to 5 paying clients or paid pilots.
- Clear path to 10 clients.
- Known best-fit segment.
- Repeatable demo-to-pilot motion.
- Product priorities tied to paid conversion.

## 12. Founder Non-Negotiables

- No week without outreach.
- No lead without follow-up.
- No demo without a next step.
- No pilot without owner, scope, success criteria, and review date.
- No custom feature promises without clear commercial value.
- No free pilot unless the reference value is explicit.
- No enterprise sales theater before first 10 paying clients.
- No hiding in product work when pipeline is empty.
- Revenue over vanity.
- Proof over polish.
